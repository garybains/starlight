Let’s face it, there is a lot of information out there. A LOT! How do you know what is true? You have to ask the questions. From everything I have read, I know that there is more than one definition for Cry It Out, Co-sleeping, sleeping through the night, sleep training infants. The only way to know if your sleep consultant is on the same page as you is to ask what their definitions of terms are.

Co-sleeping: Some books see this as sleeping on the same surface as the baby, while for others it is a separate surface that is attached to the bed. Some even consider co-sleeping as sharing the same room. I consider co-sleeping, sleeping on the same surface or a surface that is attached to the bed. The Canadian Pediatric Society recommends room sharing for the first 6 months, but does not recommend bed sharing because there are too many what if’s. Think of it as you telling me you have a pool and asking me if you can dive in. There are too many unknowns, does the pool have water? Is there a deep end? Are you diving into the deep end? have you recently eaten? Do you know how to swim? Even with these questions answered there are still others that may not have been asked and the information may not have been given, maybe the cover is on? Because the list of questions regarding safe sleep is long, and each circumstance is different it is simply safer to avoid bedsharing.

Sleeping through the night: Some people view this as actually sleeping through the night; 6 pm–6 am or 7 pm–7 am. Some view it as sleeping 5 hours!!!! I view co-sleeping as sleeping through the night from the time you tuck them in around 6:30-7:00 pm until 6:30-7:00 am.

Crying it out: Some feel that this is, as it sounds, letting your baby cry until they tire themselves out and fall asleep and this could take 4 hours. Some know that it implies making sure everything is fine with baby, recently fed, changed, bathed, cuddled and giving them a chance to fall asleep on their own and only responding to them at certain determined feed times. This is what I consider cry it out. With my training and my many volunteers, I have yet to use cry it out, not because I am against it, but because I didn’t feel it was the right fit for the families I have worked with so far. Am I for or against certain training methods? Not as long as it’s being used properly and safely and is a good fit for both parents and baby.

Sleep training for little ones: Some think you can’t sleep train infants, 3 months or less. Yes and no, not in the same way you sleep train older babies and children, but you can assist their sleep. Less than 3 weeks, if they are struggling with sleep it is sometimes related to how they feed or other medical reasons, because of this, I don’t work with infants less than 3 weeks. Less than three months old need help with settling and being soothed and comforted, there are certain cuddling and soothing techniques that are excellent for little babies, even the fussier ones who fit the bill for colic. Sleep training techniques are very age appropriate and what works for a three year old does not work for a three month old.
